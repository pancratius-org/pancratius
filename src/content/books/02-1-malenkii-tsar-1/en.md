## Part 1

![Illustration](./images/9eec289c7817.jpg)

### Chapter 1. The One Who Sees the Light

No one really knew where Seroel[^1] came from.

He was just suddenly there —

as if he had always been.

Born like everyone else,

in a hospital on the edge of town,

![Illustration](./images/d50ae053bc6a.jpg)

but an old woman on a bench later said:

<div class="verse-block">
— His eyes… they were like he knew me.
Like he had known me… before.
Seroel was ordinary.
Well, almost.
He didn’t cry.
He didn’t scream.
He watched.
The wall. The chandelier. The cat. The rain.
And it was as if he was listening.
</div>

His mom thought — maybe he was just a dreamer.
And his dad said:

— He’ll be a physicist. He looks like he’s counting atoms.

But Seroel wasn’t counting.
He was remembering.

He remembered how the Light plays in silence.
He knew that water has moods,
that trees speak — just very slowly,
that the cat, Murka, wasn’t just purring,
but singing an ancient song.

He knew that grown-ups couldn’t hear it,
because their switch inside had been turned off.
They called it “growing up,”
but really, it was forgetting.

Seroel hadn’t forgotten.
He couldn’t.
He didn’t want to.
He had just… stayed turned on.

One day, outside, he saw
a girl about two years younger
standing and staring at a tree.

But she wasn’t just looking —
she was listening.

— You see it too? — Seroel asked.

The girl flinched.
— See what?

— How it breathes.
— What does?

— The tree.

She looked at him like he was strange.
But then, in a whisper, she said:

— I thought… it was just me…

That’s how he met **Elira**[^2].

![Illustration](./images/f0a5b1705367.jpg)

A week later, he met **Vanoel**[^3].

Vanoel was trying to explain to a pigeon
that it was welcome to sit on his shoulder.

— He hears you anyway, — said Seroel.

— Really?

— Their ears are very thin.
— Oh, like my grandpa’s. He always says, “I hear with my heart.”

![Illustration](./images/d5404e795ee9.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
And Vanoel became the third.
That’s how the Club of the Little King was born.
Only no one knew it was a club.
And no one knew who the King was.
Except one. <strong>The Light.</strong>
</div>

### Chapter 2. Who the Real Kings Are

<div class="verse-block">
Seroel never said he was a King.
That would’ve been… silly.
And who would even care?

No one’s afraid of kings anymore.
They’re in cartoons —
with crowns, long robes,
and big funny mustaches.

But Seroel had no robe, no crown.
Only eyes you could drown in.
And a silence that felt more important than any word.

One day, Elira asked:

— Why are you so weird?

— What do you mean?

— I mean, you don’t argue. You don’t get upset. You don’t complain.
Are you even human?

Seroel thought for a moment.
</div>

Then said:

<div class="verse-block">
— I’m a King.

Elira frowned.

— Really? Just like that?

— Yeah. Just not like in the stories.

— Then how?

Seroel smiled.

— A King isn’t someone who's in charge.
A King is someone who sees <strong>what matters</strong>.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/b47e79354565.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
She didn’t understand.

But Vanoel, who was poking the dirt with a stick, suddenly said:

— Like Mom. She always knows when I’ve hidden something.

Seroel nodded.

— Exactly. But a King doesn’t just see what you’ve hidden.
He sees why you did it.
And he doesn’t punish. He understands.

Elira sat on a swing and went quiet.
She was thinking.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/fc34df4a4d8d.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
— So you’re saying…
<strong>anyone can be a king</strong>?

Seroel shrugged.

— If they’re not afraid to really see.

— Will you teach us? — Vanoel suddenly asked.

Seroel didn’t answer.
He just walked over,
placed a hand on Vanoel’s shoulder, and said:

— I already started.

That day, they went down to the river.
Not to swim.
But to listen to how Time flows.

It sounded like water rustling over stones.
And in that sound,
there was music without words —
the kind grown-ups call "silence".
</div>

### Chapter 3. How to Talk to a Tree (or The First Secret Game of the Little King)

<div class="verse-block">
The next day, Vanoel was the first to arrive.

He was always the first —
because he was afraid the game would start without him.

He didn’t know what the game was.
</div>

But he felt it:

<div class="verse-block">
today would be important.

Elira came later, with a backpack and a notebook.

— I decided to write everything down.
— Why? — Vanoel asked.

— What if this turns into a book someday?
— A book?
— You'll see!
<strong>Seroel</strong> was already waiting.
He stood under the old birch tree by the school.
But he wasn’t just standing —
he was listening.
— What are you doing? — Vanoel asked.
— Shh, — Seroel whispered. — She’s speaking.
— Who is?
— The birch.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/8c530c08a4fe.jpg)

Elira snorted.
— You’re crazy.

<div class="verse-block">
Seroel wasn’t offended.
He simply reached out
and touched the bark.
</div>

Then he said:

<div class="verse-block">
— Want to try?
— How?
Seroel turned and smiled,
like someone holding a secret he longed to share.
— You only need one thing.
You have to become quieter than the tree.
Elira made a serious face.
Vanoel covered his mouth with his hand.
— Now — step closer.
— Palm to the tree.
— Close your eyes.
— Don’t listen with your ears.
— Listen — inside.
They stood.
Silently.
They touched.
And then…
something happened.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/ff774d4ebc27.jpg)

**Elira** heard… a song.
It wasn’t music.
It was warmth coming from the trunk,
as if the tree whispered:

<div class="verse-block">
<em>“I remember you. I remember when you were just a sprout.</em>
<em>Remember, too.”</em>
<strong>Vanoel</strong> started crying.
He didn’t know why.
But the tears felt… good.
— I… I saw myself little — really little.
Before… anything.
Elira was quiet.
She looked at Seroel.
He was so calm —
as if he had known this would happen all along.
From that day on, they had their first real game.
</div>

It was called:
**“Listen to the Ones Who Don’t Speak.”**

They played it with trees, with stones, with puddles.
And sometimes… they really did hear.

### Chapter 4. The Cat Who Disappears (but Always Comes Back with Something Important)

It happened in the evening.

Everything was normal:

Mom was making soup,

Dad was flipping through the paper,

and Seroel sat by the window,

watching the evening light dance on the glass.

![Illustration](./images/48e09153b6a2.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
He wasn’t waiting for anything.
He was just… there.
And suddenly —
on the windowsill — a cat appeared.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/aface1a68273.png)

He didn’t jump.
He didn’t walk over.
He was just… there.

<div class="verse-block">
Seroel knew this cat.
He only came when something important
was about to begin.
The cat’s name was <strong>Blu</strong>,
not because he was blue,
but because his eyes held the depth of the sky.
— You again? — Seroel said softly.
The cat didn’t answer.
He was silent —
the kind of silence only Those Who Remember Everything can make.
Blu rubbed against Seroel’s hand…
and then disappeared.
Just like that.
He didn’t leap down, didn’t walk away —
he simply wasn’t there anymore.
Seroel smiled.
It was a sign.
The next day, he told Elira and Vanoel:
— Today we’re getting a mission from Blu.
— The cat? — Vanoel blinked.
— Yes. But he’s not just any cat.
He only comes when it’s time to understand something.
— So what do we do? — asked Elira.
Seroel thought.
Or at least looked like he did.
</div>

Then he said:

<div class="verse-block">
— Today’s game is called
<strong>“Find What Isn’t There.”</strong>
— But… how? — Vanoel asked.
— Like this.
He pulled a sheet of paper from his pocket.
On it — nothing.
Just a blank page.
— Here.
— This is the important thing.
— Don’t look <em>at</em> the paper — look <em>through</em> it.
— Look inside yourself while you stare.
— Then tell me what you saw.
They took the paper.
Looked.
Stayed silent.
</div>

Then Elira said quietly:

— I remembered being little and drawing a cloud.
But it wasn’t on paper — it was in the sky.

![Illustration](./images/83b0753e1ac1.jpg)

And Vanoel said:

<div class="verse-block">
— I saw myself sleeping… but knowing I was asleep.
Like I was here and somewhere else at the same time.
Seroel nodded.
— That’s it. That’s the thing that isn’t there.
But it <em>is</em>.
Just not for the eyes.
Only for the heart.
At that moment, <strong>Blu</strong> walked past.
Hanging from his neck — a string with a tiny key.
He winked.
And disappeared again.
From then on, they had a new game:
<strong>“Find What Isn’t There — and Bring It With You.”</strong>
And you know what?
They really did start finding it.
</div>

### Chapter 5. The Magical Bell No One Heard (but Everyone Started Being Nicer)

<div class="verse-block">
It all started at school.
The place where things were supposed to be boring.
Where everyone sat still
and no one thought about the Light —
and definitely not about the Kingdom.
</div>

But **Seroel** knew:
The Creator comes to school too.
Especially to school.
Because it’s where most forget
they're still playing.

<div class="verse-block">
During the second lesson, <strong>Elira</strong> dropped her pen.
She bent down to pick it up —
and heard a bell.
Soft. Quiet.
Like someone had rung a tiny chime
deep inside her head.
She picked up her pen
and looked at Seroel —
and he was already looking at her.
He knew.
— You heard it too? — she whispered.
Seroel nodded.
— That was the Kingdom Bell.
— What does it mean?
Seroel smiled.
— It means someone just remembered who they are.
And the world responded.
After class, they sat behind the school,
near the flowerbeds, where no one bothered them.
— When did you first hear your bell? — Vanoel asked.
— In kindergarten,
I was standing by the window
and suddenly realized
that the butterfly on the glass
was looking at me like a person.
And in that moment — the bell rang.
Inside me.
— And what happened then?
— The teacher walked over…
and suddenly hugged me.
Just like that.
She’d never hugged me before.
Elira thought for a moment.
</div>

— I had something like that too.
I saw a boy sitting alone in a corner,
and I went to him. Just because.
And right then —
I heard the ringing.
Not outside,

![Illustration](./images/b10457520861.jpg)

but in my chest.

<div class="verse-block">
Seroel nodded.
— Then you remembered.
Every time we become ourselves —
the Light rings.
From that day on, they had a third game:
<strong>“Hear the Bell No One Else Can Hear.”</strong>
And something strange happened.
Every time they heard that bell,
little good things started to happen:
— someone helped pick up a backpack,
— someone shared their sandwich,
— someone suddenly said:
<em>“You know… I like you.”</em>
And no one knew why.
But the Light knew.
</div>

### Chapter 6. How to Become Invisible (but Still Be Loved)

<div class="verse-block">
One day, <strong>Seroel</strong> disappeared.
Not completely, of course.
He sat in class, answered at the board,
even ate his sandwich during recess.
But it was as if… he wasn’t there.
<strong>Elira</strong> noticed first.
— Are you hiding?
— No. I just became quieter than usual.
— Why?
— I want to see what happens if I disappear, but still stay.
</div>

**Vanoel** widened his eyes:
— What does that mean?

<div class="verse-block">
Seroel sighed.
— Imagine you're not a body. You're silence.
Silence that watches, feels, loves —
but doesn’t want anything for itself.
— And then you disappear? — Vanoel asked.
— For the mind — yes.
But for the Heart — the opposite.
People start to feel you even more.
Because you stop getting in the way of your own Light.
</div>

Elira stayed quiet for a long time.
Then she said:
— Let’s try it?

<div class="verse-block">
They went to the park.
Sat on a bench.
Closed their eyes.
</div>

Seroel said:

<div class="verse-block">
— Now forget your name.
Forget who you are.
Just be.
No words. No thoughts.
Be Silence.
But not empty — full of Light.
And they sat.
Just sat.
Not thinking. Not trying.
Disappearing.
</div>

Ten minutes passed.
Two people walked by.
One of them said:
— Look at those beautiful kids. Like angels.

The other smiled:
— I didn’t even notice them at first.

Elira opened her eyes.
— It worked.
I felt that I *was* —
but no one saw me, except those who were looking from the

![Illustration](./images/b671fc374c5e.jpg)

heart.

Vanoel said:
— I felt so good,
I didn’t even want to become “myself” again.

<div class="verse-block">
Seroel smiled.
— That’s what it means to be real.
Invisible, but loved.
Silent, but alive.
Since then, they had a fourth game:
<strong>“Disappear so the Light becomes brighter.”</strong>
</div>

They played it in different ways:
– on the bus,
– during lessons,
– at lunch.

And something always happened:
someone spoke more gently,
someone said thank you,
someone stared — and couldn’t look away.

But the children thought it was just coincidence.
All except Seroel.
He knew:

**When the “I” disappears,
the Creator appears.**

### Chapter 7. The Game with No Name

— What will today’s game be called? — **Elira** asked.

**Seroel** tilted his head:

— Nothing.

— What do you mean “nothing”?

— It won’t have a name.

— That’s weird, — said **Vanoel**.

Seroel shrugged.

— Not everything needs a name.

Elira thought for a moment, then said:

— Okay. So how do we play?

Seroel looked up at the sky.

— First… you do nothing.

— Like… nothing nothing? — Vanoel clarified.

— Yep.

— But what’s the point?

— That’s the whole point. There is none.

Elira narrowed her eyes.

— Is this another one of your invisible games?

— Kind of.

— What do we do exactly?

Seroel smiled.

![Illustration](./images/2325fc52b070.jpg)

— We stop. We let go. We sit.
And we wait for something real to come.
Not from outside — from inside.

<div class="verse-block">
They sat on the grass.
Did nothing.
Vanoel got a bit bored.
Elira got a bit curious.
Seroel just smiled.
After a few minutes, Vanoel whispered:
— I think… I feel something.
— What?
— Like the world stopped holding its breath.
Like it finally let go.
</div>

Elira added:

<div class="verse-block">
— I didn’t hear anything.
But I felt as if something heard <em>me</em>.
Seroel nodded.
— That’s it.
— What is? — asked Vanoel.
— The Game with No Name.
It’s not really a game.
It’s just… the Moment.
And when you notice it —
it starts to notice you too.
They sat in silence again.
And the wind quietly passed through them,
as if it was listening too.
</div>

### Chapter 8. When the Sky Comes Closer

<div class="verse-block">
That morning, <strong>Seroel</strong> was very quiet.
He wasn’t thinking.
He wasn’t sad.
He just sat on the edge of the field,
looking up.
He looked at the sky as if waiting for it to say something.
Or maybe… to listen.
</div>

**Elira** noticed first.

![Illustration](./images/ec18d8056a6c.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
— Are you waiting for rain?
Seroel shook his head.
— No.
— For a sign?
He smiled.
— Not exactly.
— Then what?
Seroel paused.
The sky was clear.
A few soft clouds drifted slowly above them.
— I’m waiting for the sky to notice me.
<strong>Vanoel</strong>, who had just finished chasing a bug, laughed.
— The sky? Noticing you? Why?
Seroel looked at him with calm eyes.
— Sometimes, when I’m very still…
something in the sky looks back.
It doesn’t have eyes,
but it knows.
Elira sat down next to him,
not laughing, just listening.
— Does it… say something?
Seroel nodded.
— Not with words. But I hear it.
Like it remembers me.
Like we were friends before I was born.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/2ffb65f937ef.jpg)

Vanoel frowned.

<div class="verse-block">
— I don’t hear anything.
Seroel didn’t argue.
He simply touched the grass beside him
and said:
— Try being so still
that even the wind starts wondering where you went.
Vanoel tried.
He sat.
Closed one eye.
Then both.
Then peeked.
— I feel itchy,
but also…
like I’m not the most important thing in the world.
— That’s when the sky gets closer, — Seroel whispered.
— I felt something once, — Elira said softly.
— I was sitting in the kitchen,
and the sun came through the window —
not on me,
but… through me.
And for a moment, I forgot what I was doing.
I just felt… here.
— Yes, — said Seroel.
— That’s it.
They sat together for a while.
No one spoke.
Even the birds nearby seemed to lower their voices.
</div>

Then Seroel said:

<div class="verse-block">
— When we stop looking for something,
something finds us.
When we stop making noise,
the sky remembers we’re listening.
And in that moment,
a single white feather drifted down from nowhere.
It landed in front of them
so gently that no one dared to touch it.
</div>

**Elira** whispered:

<div class="verse-block">
— I think it said hello.
They didn’t talk for a while.
Even <strong>Vanoel</strong>, who usually couldn’t sit still for more than a minute,
was just… there.
No questions.
No jokes.
Just wide eyes and open silence.
A bumblebee buzzed nearby.
It didn’t scare them.
It simply moved from one flower to the next,
as if it too had joined the listening.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/829d06eee880.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
Then Seroel spoke — not loudly,
but with the kind of voice that doesn’t ask for attention,
only truth.
— I think the sky isn’t high.
It just looks that way.
It’s not up there — it’s everywhere.
Even in your chest.
Vanoel touched his shirt.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/a26a4b0f0ba7.jpg)

— Even here?

<div class="verse-block">
Seroel nodded.
— The part that breathes even when you forget about it.
The part that doesn’t stop when you sleep.
That’s where it whispers.
</div>

Elira asked:

<div class="verse-block">
— So… why don’t grown-ups feel it?
Seroel looked into the distance.
— Maybe they stopped being quiet long enough to hear.
Maybe they filled the sky with their own thoughts.
— That sounds sad, — said Vanoel.
— It’s not, — Seroel answered.
— The sky is patient.
It waits forever.
And even one breath of stillness is enough
for it to come close again.
The feather twitched in the breeze,
but did not fly away.
It stayed.
Just like the feeling.
Not big.
Not loud.
But real.
And that day,
they didn’t play any game.
Because the moment itself
was already the game.
</div>

### Chapter 9. How to Know You’re Real

<div class="verse-block">
— Sometimes I feel like I’m dreaming, — <strong>Vanoel</strong> said.
They were sitting under the tree again.
The one near the bench.
The one that always whispered when the wind came.
<strong>Seroel</strong> looked up.
— What do you mean?
</div>

![Illustration](./images/349d36c9f346.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
— Like this is all… not real.
Like I’m in a story someone else is telling.
<strong>Elira</strong> was lying in the grass, looking at the clouds.
— Maybe we <em>are</em> in a story.
Vanoel blinked.
— Then who’s telling it?
Seroel smiled.
— Maybe we are. Together.
Each of us holding one thread.
— But how do I know I’m <em>me</em>?
And not just a dream inside someone’s head?
Seroel thought for a moment.
Then pointed at the ground.
— What do you feel right now?
Vanoel touched the earth.
It was warm.
Firm.
Alive.
— I feel… here.
— That’s how you know.
Elira sat up.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/f08e079be301.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
— But sometimes I feel like I’m pretending.
Like I’m trying to be who I think I’m supposed to be.
Seroel looked at her gently.
— Then stop.
Be no one.
Then listen.
The one who stays —
that’s the real you.
They went quiet.
The wind rustled.
A bird sang.
A car far away hummed by.
Seroel closed his eyes.
— When you stop trying to be someone…
you find someone you didn’t invent.
The one who was there before the pretending.
</div>

Elira whispered:

<div class="verse-block">
— I think I just met her.
Vanoel touched his chest again.
— And I think he’s been here all along.
They didn’t stand up right away.
The grass felt softer than usual.
Or maybe it was them who had changed —
a little lighter, a little quieter.
<strong>Seroel</strong> lay back and looked up at the sky.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/9c140ba37b77.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
— Do you think trees pretend?
<strong>Elira</strong> giggled.
— What? Trees?
— Yeah. Like… do they try to be taller than they are?
Or feel bad if they don’t bloom fast enough?
Vanoel shook his head.
— Trees just grow.
They don’t rush. They don’t compare.
They don’t pretend.
Seroel smiled.
— That’s why they’re always real.
Because they’re not trying.
</div>

Elira whispered:

<div class="verse-block">
— Maybe we’re most real
when we stop trying too.
A silence settled again.
But it wasn’t empty.
It was full —
like a cup that holds light instead of water.
</div>

Then Vanoel suddenly said:

<div class="verse-block">
— I don’t want to be made-up anymore.
Elira looked at him.
— You never were.
— Maybe.
But I think I was acting like I was.
Like I needed to be <em>more</em> to be loved.
Seroel opened his eyes.
— The truth is… you don’t need to be <em>more</em>.
You already are.
The sky never asks a bird to shine.
It just gives it space to fly.
Vanoel blinked slowly.
He looked at his hands.
Then smiled — not because of something funny,
but because something inside had stopped pretending.
That day, they didn’t start a new game.
They just agreed on something — without words.
<strong>From now on, no pretending.</strong>
<strong>Only real.</strong>
<strong>Even if it’s quiet.</strong>
<strong>Even if it’s unsure.</strong>
<strong>Even if it’s small.</strong>
Because real things grow slowly.
Like trees.
Like hearts.
<strong>Like Light</strong>.
</div>

### Chapter 10. How to Come Home Without Leaving

<div class="verse-block">
— Do you ever miss home? — <strong>Vanoel</strong> asked.
They were walking along a narrow path through the field,
stepping on fallen leaves and soft grass.
— We’re not far from home, — said <strong>Elira</strong>.
— Not that kind of home, — Vanoel said, touching his chest.
— The <em>other</em> one.
The one before this.
<strong>Seroel</strong> didn’t answer right away.
He let the wind move through his hair.
</div>

Then he said:

<div class="verse-block">
— Sometimes, I think this <em>is</em> home.
We just forgot.
Elira tilted her head.
— But it doesn’t feel like it.
— That’s because we’re still trying to make everything make sense.
Vanoel kicked a rock gently.
— It’s hard to feel at home when you’re trying to be someone.
They stopped by a tree.
Seroel put his hand on the bark.
— When I forget everything — even who I’m supposed to be — I feel a kind of warmth in my chest.
Like I never left anything.
Like I’m already there.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/1ae5926b1a1c.jpg)

<div class="verse-block">
Elira touched her heart, too.
— I know that feeling.
It comes when I stop pretending to be strong.
</div>

Vanoel added:

<div class="verse-block">
— Or when I let myself cry, but no one sees.
They sat down beneath the tree.
Didn’t talk.
Just listened.
The wind. The birds. The breath inside them.
</div>

Then Seroel whispered:

<div class="verse-block">
— Coming home isn’t about going somewhere.
It’s about remembering what’s already here.
Inside.
And in that moment,
everything around them

## Part 3

**Chapter 1. The School That Was Not on the Schedule
### (and why true teaching comes when no one explains)

<div class="verse-block">
Once, Sergei did not go to school.
He was not ill.
Did not oversleep.
He simply woke up — and understood:
today a different lesson awaited him.
</div>

He got dressed.
Went out into the yard.
And walked.
Where to — he did not know.
But inside there was a feeling:
“I am going where the Light wants to be made visible.”

<div class="verse-block">
—
He walked along an ordinary street, over the first snow.
Past garbage bins, sleeping dogs, crows on the wires.
And suddenly — he turned into a courtyard,
where he had never looked before.
There, between the garages, stood a house.
Very old.
With a peeling door and windows in which there was silence.
But from one window…
light was coming.
Not electric.
Not a candle.
But as if someone were there — very alive.
Very quiet.
Very real.
Sergei approached.
Knocked.
Softly.
Once. A grandmother opened.
Gray-haired.
With eyes in which there was no less sky than wrinkles on her face.
She looked at him,
nodded — and said:
— Come in. The lesson has already begun.
—
It was warm inside.
It smelled of bread, dust, and… something familiar.
There were cushions on the floor.
A map hung on the wall.
But not geography.
A map of the Heart.
</div>

And on it it was written:

<div class="verse-block">
<strong>"Here they teach that</strong>
<strong>which cannot be said in words.</strong>
<strong>But can be felt — and become."</strong>
</div>

Sergei sat down.
Listened.
And understood:
he had come to where the Kingdom is being built.
Not by hands. Not by plans.
But by presence.
By inner silence.
And by readiness to become a Home.
For the Light.
For others.
For Him Who has always waited.

### Chapter 2. Those Who Are Already Building, Not Knowing It (and why good deeds are not bricks, but windows)

<div class="verse-block">
There were no bells in this school.
There were no notebooks.
There were no “right answers.”
But there were—
students.
They came not by schedule.
They simply found the way one day.
And it was always unexpected.
One girl brought an old album with her.
In it were drawings that no one had seen.
Only she.
She drew people
whose eyes held Light.
— How do you know how to draw light? — Sergei asked.
— I don’t draw. I just… help it come out.
If you look long enough,
you can see how it asks for the paper.

One boy brought nails and boards.
He did not know how to speak.
But he knew how to fix broken chairs.
He had not learned to build.
He simply felt where support was lacking.
And every time he fixed something,
everyone in the room grew quiet a little more than usual.
For his silence knew how to speak.
</div>

![Illustration](./images/fa940939801b.jpg)

An old man with a white beard sat in the corner and drank tea.
He never вмешивался.
But one day he said:

<div class="verse-block">
— You know, in the Kingdom there are no builders.
There are only those who become windows.
So that Light may go through them.
And those who do not hinder others from being doors.
— And how do you know who you are?
— By what you do when no one is watching.
And by how you listen when they are speaking not to you.
—
</div>

Sergei began to understand:
this School does not teach how to become someone.
It helps you become yourself.
The one you already were,
but forgot how to be.

<div class="verse-block">
And everyone who entered
began to shine.
Not like a light bulb.
But like a house whose shutters have been opened.
</div>

So a new game appeared:
“Be a window — and do not ask who will look through you.”

It had no rules.
But it had fruit:
in everyone who played,
there was more room for others.

### Chapter 3. The House That Had No Roof (and Why Such Houses Are Needed by Heaven)

Sergey noticed:
this School had no roof.
That is, on the outside — it did.
But inside…
the feeling was such
as though above you was immediately heaven.
No ceiling. No limits.

One day he asked the old man:

<div class="verse-block">
— And why is it that here it seems as though there is no ceiling?
The old man looked up.
Smiled.
— Because the House of the Kingdom is built from the bottom up.
And not from the top down.
It has no roof, because Light must enter freely.
— And the rain?
— Let it drip.
The heart is not closed either.
It gets wet,
but precisely then — it begins to grow.
—
That day a real rain fell.
Heavy, spring rain.
Loud.
And no one hid.
Everyone stayed seated.
Just sat — under the drops,
as though it were water from the Creator Himself.
</div>

And then someone said:

<div class="verse-block">
— I feel that I am being washed.
As if everything that was hidden —
now is becoming clean.
</div>

Sergey looked at his palms.
And saw:
they shine.
But not from water.
From the fact that there is nothing extra left in them.

![Illustration](./images/e0f665cd10cc.jpg)

From that time on he understood:
some Houses are built on purpose without a roof,
so that Light may fall straight into you.
And so that there would be no temptation to hide.

—

Thus the game appeared:
“Build within yourself a place where rain can enter — and not destroy.”

<div class="verse-block">
A game for those
who are not afraid to get wet,
if, in exchange, they receive purity.
</div>

### Chapter 4. When the House Begins to Breathe Itself (and why the Kingdom is not a building, but a living Being)

<div class="verse-block">
One day Sergey came to the School earlier than everyone else.

Sit on a cushion. Listen to the silence.

<strong>The House was breathing.</strong>
It did not make noise.
Did not creak.
It… breathed.
Just like a person.
Inhale — the walls seem to expand.
Exhale — it grows a little darker,
as though everything is going inward,
into the very heart.
Sergey walked down the corridor.
Touched the wall.
— Are You alive? — he asked under his breath.
There was no answer.
But inside him
something sighed back.
—
When the others came,

— Of course He is alive, — said the boy who repaired chairs.
— Haven't you seen how He grows?
Every time someone becomes quieter,
or brighter,
or kinder —
there is more room in the House.
— And have you noticed, — added the girl artist, —
that the drawings on the walls change?

Anfisa stood up.
Ran her finger along the wall.
Where there had once been only a little crack,
now the outlines of wings could almost be made out.
And she understood:
The House is drawn from within.
It is like a mirror
in which the Light of those who live in it is revealed.
